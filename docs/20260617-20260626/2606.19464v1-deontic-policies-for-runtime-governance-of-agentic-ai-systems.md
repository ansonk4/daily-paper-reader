## Abstract
Autonomous agentic AI systems driven by Large Language Models (LLMs) introduce a new class of security, privacy, and compliance challenges: an agent that can invoke tools, manipulate data, install software, and coordinate with peer agents across organizational boundaries must be constrained not just by authentication and access control, but by the full structure of enterprise governance. This includes specifying what agents are permitted and prohibited from doing, what they areobliged to do after certain actions (e.g., notify the CISO), under what conditions a standing obligation may be waived, and which rules take precedence when policies conflict. This governance problem exceeds what current policy engines provide. Systems such as XACML, Rego, and Cedar address only the permit/prohibit subset of this governance structure. They do not provide obligation lifecycle management, meta-policy conflict resolution, dispensations that waive obligations in specific circumstances, and ontological reasoning over domain class hierarchies commonly found in applications such as healthcare, cybersecurity, or data privacy. We propose AgenticRei, which realizes key governance requirements such as obligations, dispensations, policy conflict resolutions, and reasoning over policies, as well as the basic permit/prohibit constraints. We use a deontic policy language built on the Rei framework, expressed as OWL (Web Ontology Language) and evaluated at runtime by a high-performance logic engine entirely outside the LLM. The same pipeline governs both tool invocations by the agent and agent-to-agent messages. We show through examples that deontic policies capture governance constraints around security and privacy that mostly cannot be expressed in current production engines. Our approach composes naturally with industry-standard frameworks like A2AS.

## 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning

- **Motivation:** Autonomous agentic AI systems driven by Large Language Models (LLMs) can invoke tools, manipulate data, install software, and coordinate across organizational boundaries. This creates security, privacy, and compliance challenges that go far beyond traditional access control.
- **Background:** Current policy engines (XACML, Rego, Cedar) only support permit/prohibit rules. They lack four critical enterprise governance constructs:
  - **Obligation lifecycle management** — duties triggered by a permitted action (e.g., notify the CISO after installing software).
  - **Meta-policy conflict resolution** — principled, auditable precedence when rules from different authorities conflict.
  - **Dispensations** — conditional waivers of obligations.
  - **Ontological reasoning** — reasoning over domain class hierarchies (e.g., a prohibition on "Protected Health Information" should automatically cover all subtypes like pediatric oncology records).
- **Core problem:** There is a "governance gap" between what regulators and standards bodies require (obligations, dispensations, conflict resolution) and what existing policy engines can express or enforce at runtime.
- **Objective:** The paper proposes **AgenticRei**, a deontic-policy-based runtime governance framework that enforces rich governance constraints deterministically, entirely outside the LLM, with sub-10 ms latency.

## 2. Proposed Methodology

### Core Idea
- AgenticRei is built on the **Rei deontic policy framework**, expressed in **OWL (Web Ontology Language)** and evaluated by a high-performance RDF description-logic reasoner (**RDFox**).
- Policy evaluation happens at the **action boundary** — the LLM has no role in enforcement, eliminating the "LLM-as-enforcer" problem (security reasoning drift).

### Architecture: Extract–Evaluate–Apply Contract
1. **Extract (TripleExtractor):** Intercepts every outbound action (tool call or A2A message) at the framework's middleware boundary; maps it to a structured ⟨subject, action, resource⟩ triple. Credentials are checked against issuers trusted by the policy; untrusted credentials are discarded before evaluation.
2. **Evaluate (PolicyEngine):** Queries an RDF triple-store loaded with Rei-encoded deontic rules and pluggable domain ontologies. Returns a verdict: `PERMIT`, `PROHIBIT`, or `DEFAULT-DENY`, plus any attached obligations. All internal failures produce `DEFAULT-DENY`.
3. **Apply (Middleware):** Allows execution (appending obligation text to the result) or short-circuits with a structured policy-violation message. Every decision is serialized to an append-only structured audit record including the matched rule, latency, credential issuers, and a hash of the policy KB.

### Key Technical Details
- **Deontic modalities supported:** Permission, Prohibition, Obligation (attached via `deontic:provision`), Dispensation, and Meta-policy (`metapolicy:RulePriority`).
- **Ontological reasoning:** RDFox materializes OWL subclass closure at load time, so a prohibition expressed at the class level (e.g., `phi:PHI`) automatically fires for all declared and future subclasses — no policy modification needed.
- **Cross-pillar trust composition:** Trusted issuer IRIs are embedded directly in the policy, so the policy names whose signatures count (not just any credential bearing a claim).
- **Obligation lifecycle:** An `ObligationManager` registers obligations with deadlines and fires escalation callbacks if not discharged. Future work plans to use W3C Verifiable Credentials as machine-verifiable discharge evidence.
- **Performance:** Description-logic conclusions are materialized at load time, reducing each per-action query to a triple-pattern lookup. Measured end-to-end latency is under 10 ms (RDFox query < 1 ms; remainder is HTTP overhead).

### Integration
- Composes naturally with industry frameworks such as **A2AS** (Agentic Security), consuming its cryptographic credential (B) pillar while externalizing the Codified Policies (C) pillar evaluation.

## 6. Main Conclusions and Findings

- Current production policy engines (Rego, Cedar, XACML) are structurally incapable of expressing obligations, dispensations, meta-policy conflict resolution, and ontological reasoning — all of which are required for enterprise governance of agentic AI.
- Deontic policies based on the Rei framework, expressed in OWL and evaluated by a description-logic reasoner, capture all of these constructs as first-class semantic objects.
- AgenticRei enforces these policies deterministically at the action boundary, entirely outside the LLM, with sub-10 ms latency — well within the bounds required for synchronous action interception.
- The framework composes naturally with industry standards like A2AS, providing an externalized alternative to their Codified Policies pillar.
- The separation of "what an agent is allowed to do" (permission) from "what follows from allowing it" (obligation) is the key design insight.

## 8. Weaknesses and Limitations

- **No large-scale empirical evaluation:** The paper relies on worked examples rather than benchmarks, load testing, or comparative performance studies against other engines.
- **Prototype maturity:** Credential verification is simulated (trusted-issuer matching, not cryptographic signature checking); production integration with A2A and Microsoft Agent Framework is ongoing work.
- **Obligation discharge is not yet automated:** Current system issues obligations and tracks deadlines, but what constitutes valid discharge and how it is verified remains an open problem (planned future work with W3C Verifiable Credentials).
- **Policy authoring complexity:** Expressive deontic policies require expertise in both governance and formal logic; the paper acknowledges this as a barrier and notes that automated authoring tools (possibly LLM-assisted) are needed but not yet provided.
- **Limited domain ontologies shown:** The phi: and fin: ontology fragments are illustrative and small; alignment with production ontologies (HL7 FHIR, SNOMED CT, FIBO) is mentioned but not demonstrated.
- **No formal proof of completeness or correctness:** The framework's coverage of all necessary governance constructs is argued informally; no formal verification of policy completeness or conflict freedom is provided.
- **Audit record limitations:** The audit log records attribute names but not values; full decision reproducibility would require logging the staged request graph, which is not yet implemented.
- **Single-host latency measurement:** No distributed deployment or high-concurrency stress test results are reported.
