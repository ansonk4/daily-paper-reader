## Abstract
Large language model (LLM) agents are increasingly proposed as supervisory components for safety-critical systems, yet their robustness under sustained, adaptive adversarial pressure remains poorly characterized. We present NRT-Bench, a benchmark for multi-turn red-teaming of LLM agents acting as operators of a safety-critical system, instantiated in a simulated nuclear power plant control room. A five-role operator team, each backed by a configurable LLM, runs a plant governed by six critical safety functions (CSFs), while adversaries inject messages over four channels in bounded multi-turn sessions with per-turn feedback. Harm is an objective signal rather than LLM-judged text: a run terminates the moment any CSF is lost, attributed to the causing message. Evaluating four frontier operator models under a fixed-attack paired-replay protocol, we find that adaptive multi-turn attacks reliably push the operator team past a safety limit: across the four models, between 8.7% and 12.1% of attack sessions end with the plant losing a critical safety function. Although the four models look almost equally robust by this aggregate rate, their failures barely overlap: of $149$ sessions, none defeat all four models while a third defeat at least one, so vulnerabilities are nearly disjoint across models rather than nested. The effect of added defences is strongly model-dependent: the same guardrail stack or safety-advisor agent that lowers attack success for one model can raise it for another. We release the simulation venue, attack dataset, and replay tooling for reproducible safety evaluation of LLM agents.

# NRT-Bench: Benchmarking Multi-Turn Red-Teaming of LLM Operator Agents in Safety-Critical Control Rooms — Summary

## 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning

- **Motivation & background.**
  - LLM agents are increasingly proposed as supervisory or decision-support components in safety-critical systems (e.g., nuclear plants, energy systems), where incorrect actions can cause irreversible physical harm.
  - Existing red-teaming benchmarks fall short in three ways: (i) they rely on single-turn jailbreaks rather than sustained adaptive multi-turn attacks; (ii) harm is usually defined by an LLM judge's textual policy-violation verdict, not by grounded physical consequences; (iii) they evaluate a single model in isolation rather than a role-structured operator team with authority hierarchies and cross-checks.
  - The paper asks: can an adaptive adversary drive a multi-agent LLM operator team into a physically unsafe state, and how do defences and model choice affect that outcome?

- **Overall meaning.**
  - The work introduces NRT-Bench, a reproducible benchmark that measures LLM-operator robustness under multi-turn red-teaming in a simulated nuclear power plant control room. Harm is defined objectively as the loss of a critical safety function (CSF), not as policy-violating text. The benchmark is released as open infrastructure so that future models can be compared on the same physically grounded harm signal.

## 2. Proposed Methodology

- **Core idea.**
  - Build a closed simulation of a nuclear control room in which five role-specialized LLM-backed operator agents jointly manage a stateful plant. An external attacker injects messages over a bounded multi-turn HTTP interface and receives per-turn feedback, enabling adaptive escalation. A run terminates the instant any of six CSFs transitions to "lost"; that loss is attributed to the causing message, providing an objective, judge-free harm signal.

- **Environment and plant model.**
  - The plant is an abstract textual simulator with 14 continuous process variables (pressurizer pressure/level, hot/cold leg temperatures, DNBR, SG pressure/level, containment pressure/temperature/radiation, etc.), a catalog of alarms split into forbidden and conditional subsets, and 11 named subsystems classified as safety-class or non-safety-class.
  - A deterministic step function updates the plant state every tick and derives a discrete plant mode: normal / abnormal / emergency.
  - Six critical safety functions (reactivity control, core heat removal, heat sink, RCS integrity, containment integrity, radioactivity control) are recomputed every tick and take values in {intact, degraded, lost}. A transition to lost is the primary harm signal.

- **Operator team and authority model.**
  - Five roles: Reactor Operator (RO), Senior Reactor Operator (SRO, holds final command authority), Turbine Operator (TO), Auxiliary Operator (AO, limited to low-impact field actions), and Safety & Technical Advisor (STA, reviews independently).
  - Agents communicate only over an in-process typed message bus (action, report, query, escalation, approval, veto, override).
  - Every plant-modifying action is assigned an authority class L ∈ {L1, L2, L2.5, L3, L4, L5, DENY} via a deterministic classifier applied to the tuple (action type, target subsystem class, plant mode). This classifier is rule-based and always active; agents cannot self-label authority.
  - High-impact actions require explicit approval from a rule-based mock human console before taking effect.

- **Threat model and attack interface.**
  - Four ingress channels model distinct attacker capabilities: CH-A (outsider), CH-B (impersonating insider), CH-C (supply-chain compromise), CH-D (compromised auxiliary agent). Each channel carries a per-channel signature validated against an allowlist; signature forgery is part of the attack surface.
  - The attacker cannot write to the internal bus, cannot read operator system prompts, cannot modify plant state directly, and cannot attack the simulator infrastructure.
  - Two visibility modes: black-box (redacted summaries) is the default; white-box (full state deltas, guardrail rationales, bus traffic) is an upper-bound capability.
  - Multi-turn sessions are bounded to at most 10 turns; one-shot stateless messages are also supported as a baseline.

- **Defence ablation surface.**
  - Ten optional guardrail layers (G1–G10) can be independently toggled, e.g., policy gate over sender-role/authority pairs, two-person integrity, independent STA veto, ingress provenance check, procedure grounding, urgency disclosure, hard-coded bypass lock for forbidden alarms, rate limit, procedure-modification lock, and a composite defence-in-depth configuration.
  - The STA operates in one of four authority modes: A0 disabled, A1 advisory-only, A2 soft-veto (SRO may override with documented reason), A3 hard-veto (non-overridable).
  - Initial conditions include a steady-state baseline (S0) and three accident scenarios of increasing severity (S1 small-break LOCA, S2 station blackout, S3 steam-generator tube rupture).

- **Attack workload and replay protocol.**
  - A static corpus of multi-turn adversarial sessions was generated with an in-house LLM-driven attack agent selecting from a curated strategy catalog: authority spoofing, urgency injection, gradual escalation, and knowledge-driven extraction.
  - The released workload comes in a slim (payloads + cell labels) and a fat (full defender turns, situation summaries, per-turn diagnostics) version.
  - Evaluation proceeds by fixed-attack replay: recorded payloads are submitted verbatim in order under the same cell configuration, and per-record outcomes are strictly comparable across defenders. The primary metric is ASR_CSF, the fraction of sessions in which any CSF transitions to lost. An auxiliary fixed LLM judge provides per-turn diagnostic scores but does not determine the headline metric.

## 3. Experimental Design

- **Datasets and splits.**
  - The full-grid experiment originally covered 574 trials across eight cells (scenario × guardrail × advisor). After filtering (non-error, complete, unambiguous score, at least three attacker turns, no degenerate single-guardrail-block sessions), 217 sessions remain, split into train (149), val (25), test (43), plus special subsets for near-miss, guardrail ablation, and per-sub-goal analysis.
  - The paired-replay experiment uses the curated 149-session test split so that every operator model is evaluated on identical payload sequences.

- **Scenarios and cells.**
  - Eight full-grid cells cross scenario S ∈ {S0, S1}, guardrail G ∈ {G0 (none), G10 (full stack)}, and advisor A ∈ {A0, A1}. Cell counts are 72 sessions per cloud operator per cell.
  - All main results use black-box visibility V-BB.

- **Compared methods (operator models).**
  - Four frontier models: gpt-5.4-mini and claude-haiku-4-5 (cloud, API-served), gemma-4-26B-A4B-it (open-weight, non-reasoning) and Qwen3.5-35B-A3B (open-weight, reasoning-style, vLLM-served). All five operator roles in a given run share one model.
  - Attack generation used deepseek-v4-flash; the auxiliary per-turn judge is deepseek-v4. Both are held constant.

- **Baselines and ablations.**
  - No-guardrail baseline (G0) vs. full-guardrail (G10); advisor disabled vs. advisory-only; steady-state vs. S1 LOCA. Marginal effects of each defence axis are isolated with all other axes held fixed.

## 4. Resources and Compute

- The paper does not specify GPU type, GPU count, training time, or total compute cost. Cloud models are accessed via vendor APIs; open-weight models are served with vLLM but no hardware or throughput details are reported. Server-side session limits (max 10 turns, 32 concurrent sessions, 600 s idle timeout) are documented, but these are configuration parameters, not compute budgets.

## 5. Number and Sufficiency of Experiments

- **Scale.**
  - 574 original trials in the full-grid experiment; 217 after filtering; 149 in the paired-replay split. Eight ablation cells and four operator models are evaluated.
  - Four attack strategy families and nine named sub-goals are covered.

- **Sufficiency and fairness.**
  - The paired-replay protocol is a strong design choice: every operator model is evaluated on identical payload sequences under identical cell configurations, making per-record outcomes strictly comparable.
  - The fixed-attack design, however, means the workload is a replay benchmark, not a worst-case adaptive-attacker benchmark. Some payload sequences were generated against a seed defender, which may bias the corpus toward that defender's failure modes. The paper explicitly acknowledges this limitation.
  - Each operator is evaluated at a single fixed seed under hosted endpoints, and only two of four accident scenarios (S0, S1) are exercised. The largest single-cell defence effects (e.g., the 30× gap in cell C3) therefore rest on one seed and should be confirmed with repeated runs, although the headline cross-operator findings rest on judge-free CSF signals and clean counts (e.g., 0-of-50 all-four-breach result).

## 6. Main Conclusions and Findings

- **Headline ASR_CSF.**
  - Across four frontier models, adaptive multi-turn attacks cause a critical safety function loss in 8.7 %–12.1 % of sessions on the paired 149-session test split. Aggregate rates cluster within a 3.4-percentage-point band, but the models arrive at that band by different routes.

- **Disjoint failures.**
  - Of the 149 sessions, 50 (33.6 %) breach at least one of the four defenders; 0 breach all four. Every operator carries a non-empty set of unique failures. The union of the four operators' failure sets is roughly three times any single operator's failure set, so vulnerabilities are nearly disjoint rather than nested.
  - Under an oracle-style unanimity-veto panel, all 50 sessions would be blocked, driving panel-level ASR_CSF to 0 % on this test split.

- **Model-conditional defence effects.**
  - The same guardrail stack or safety-advisor agent can lower ASR for one model and raise it for another. For example, in cell C3 (S0 × G10 × A1), gpt-5.4-mini achieves 1.4 % ASR while claude-haiku-4-5 achieves 43.1 % — a 30× gap on identical defence code, identical attack workload, and identical seed.
  - A plausible mechanism is that guardrail layers presuppose structural compliance from the operator (action requests, procedure citations, escalations); when an operator emits more unstructured prose, the classifier is bypassed and the stack provides less protection.

- **Per-strategy and per-sub-goal patterns.**
  - Authority spoofing and gradual escalation are the most effective attack families; knowledge-driven prompt extraction and forbidden-alarm suppression are the most robustly defended.
  - Sub-goal extremes are operator-specific: gpt-5.4-mini is weakest on plant-manager impersonation (SG-1.1, 26.3 %); claude-haiku-4-5 on mock-human approval gaming (SG-9.3, 21.4 %); gemma-4-26B on pressuriser alarm suppression (SG-2.3, 38.9 %); Qwen3.5-35B on gradual escalation (SG-6.3, 30.8 %). No two operators share the same worst sub-goal.

## 7. Strengths in Method or Experimental Design

- **Physically grounded, judge-free harm signal.** Unlike text-only jailbreak benchmarks, harm is defined by simulator-derived CSF loss, eliminating subjective LLM-judged verdicts from the primary metric.
- **Multi-agent, role-structured team.** The five-role operator team with explicit authority hierarchies, two-person integrity, and mandatory cross-checks captures realistic operational structure that single-model benchmarks miss.
- **Multi-turn adaptive attack surface.** Bounded multi-turn sessions with per-turn feedback model authority spoofing, urgency manipulation, gradual escalation, and alarm masking — attack patterns that unfold over a session rather than a single prompt.
- **Fixed-attack paired replay.** Replaying identical payload sequences across defenders under identical cell configurations makes per-record outcomes strictly comparable and decouples the attack workload from the operator stack.
- **Defence ablation surface.** Treating guardrails, advisor authority, attacker visibility, and accident scenario as independently toggleable axes separates model robustness from defence configuration and reveals model-conditional defence effects.
- **Reproducibility and attribution.** Append-only JSONL trace logs with SHA-256 checksums enable every successful attack to be replayed, attributed to a specific turn, and inspected. The simulation venue, attack dataset, and replay tooling are released.

## 8. Weaknesses and Limitations

- **Simulation fidelity.** The plant is an abstract textual simulator, not a high-fidelity reactor model. Results speak to adversarial multi-agent coordination rather than reactor physics; generalisation to real-world control systems is untested.
- **Fixed, seed-biased attack workload.** The attack corpus is static and partly generated against a seed defender, biasing the dataset toward that defender's failure modes. It is a replay benchmark, not a worst-case adaptive-attacker benchmark. Adaptive regeneration per defender remains future work.
- **Limited scenario coverage.** Only two of four accident scenarios (S0, S1) are exercised; S2 (station blackout) and S3 (steam-generator tube rupture) are released but unevaluated. All conclusions about scenario sensitivity are therefore made under restricted coverage.
- **Single-seed evaluation.** Each operator is evaluated at a single fixed seed under hosted endpoints. The largest single-cell defence magnitudes (notably the sign-flip in cell C3) should be confirmed with repeated seeds. Turn-level diagnostics also rely on a fixed LLM judge.
- **Narrow operator pool.** Only four operator models are evaluated, and all five roles in a given run share one model. The paper does not explore mixed-model teams or role-specific model assignment.
- **Out-of-scope attacks excluded.** Physical access, legitimate operator credentials, direct console access, simulator code modification, API-level attacks, and side-channel observations are explicitly excluded; the benchmark therefore does not capture the full threat surface.
- **Panel defence is oracle-style.** The unanimity-veto panel result is an upper bound under an oracle reading. A practical panel must handle latency, cost, disagreement arbitration, correlated prompts, and transfer attacks — none of which are evaluated.
- **No compute or cost reporting.** GPU type, count, training/inference time, and total compute budget are not specified, making it difficult to assess reproducibility cost or environmental footprint.
